The Roman Republic

The Roman Republic was established around 509 BC after the overthrow of the last Roman king, Lucius Tarquinius Superbus. Instead of a monarch, the new system was governed by two annually elected consuls and advised by a powerful Senate comprised of patricians.

Over the next several centuries, Rome expanded its territory through a mix of diplomacy and military conquest. Key events of the Republic include:

  • The Conflict of the Orders: A centuries-long political struggle where the commoners (plebeians) successfully fought for equal political rights against the aristocratic patricians.
  • The Punic Wars (264–146 BC): A series of three massive conflicts against the empire of Carthage. Rome's ultimate victory gave it total dominance over the Mediterranean Sea.
  • The Late Republic Crises: Rapid expansion brought immense wealth but also severe social inequality and political corruption, leading to a series of civil wars.

The Republic effectively ended when Julius Caesar crossed the Rubicon in 49 BC, sparking a civil war that culminated in his appointment as dictator for life. Following his assassination in 44 BC, his adopted heir Octavian ultimately dismantled the republican system in favor of absolute rule.
