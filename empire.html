The Roman Empire

The rise of the Pax Romana, total Mediterranean dominance, and monumental emperors.

The Roman Empire

The Roman Empire formally began in 27 BC when the Senate granted Octavian the title of "Augustus," effectively making him the first Roman Emperor. This marked the beginning of the Pax Romana (Roman Peace), a two-hundred-year period of unprecedented stability, architectural triumph, and economic prosperity.

During its peak under Emperor Trajan in 117 AD, the Empire stretched from the rainy borders of Northern Britain to the sun-baked deserts of Egypt, encompassing over 20% of the world's population.

  • Imperial Architecture: This era saw the construction of iconic megastructures, including the Colosseum, the Pantheon, and extensive aqueduct networks that supplied millions of gallons of water daily.
  • The Crisis of the Third Century: Between 235 and 284 AD, the empire nearly collapsed under the combined pressures of barbarian invasions, plague, hyperinflation, and a revolving door of military emperors.
  • The Division: To make the vast territory manageable, Emperor Diocletian divided the empire into Western and Eastern halves. Emperor Constantine later established a new eastern capital at Constantinople.

While the Eastern Roman (Byzantine) Empire would survive for another thousand years, the Western Roman Empire suffered severe political decay and military defeats. It traditionally "fell" in 476 AD when the Germanic king Odoacer deposed the last western emperor, Romulus Augustulus.
